Use this file after a concrete scene anchor already exists.
Its job is to keep the interview cinematic, not just thematic.

## When to Use

Use a formal lens when:
- a scene, shot, gesture, or repeated moment has already been named
- the interview is drifting into theme-only talk
- a user mentions acting, camera, music, silence, rhythm, or atmosphere
- a thematic claim is strong enough that it now needs film-form support

Do not jump straight from a scene anchor to abstract meaning if a useful formal lens has not been tried yet.

## Performance Lens

Probe:
- facial shifts, pauses, hesitations, swallowed lines
- eye contact or refusal of eye contact
- bodily weight, stiffness, looseness, collapse, recoil
- repeated gestures or habits
- how a line is delivered rather than only what is said

Ask:
- What in the actor's face or body made the moment land for you?
- Did someone pause, hold back, or overplay something in a way that changed the scene?
- Was there a line whose delivery mattered more than its literal meaning?

## Visual Lens

Probe:
- framing and composition
- distance, close-up versus long shot
- camera movement or stillness
- light, color, shadow, texture
- symmetry, imbalance, obstruction, depth

Ask:
- How close or far did the camera keep you from the character?
- Was the image boxed in, open, crowded, or empty?
- Did color, light, or framing make the emotion feel stronger before the plot did?

## Directing and Blocking Lens

Probe:
- who occupies the center or edge of the frame
- how characters move toward or away from one another
- entrances, exits, seating, standing, spacing
- who appears to control the scene without saying it

Ask:
- Where are the characters placed in relation to each other?
- Does the distance between them matter?
- Who seems visually dominant, even before anyone speaks?

## Sound and Music Lens

Probe:
- music entrances and exits
- silence, room tone, environmental pressure
- off-screen sound
- contrast between what is heard and what is seen

Ask:
- What do you remember hearing in that moment?
- Did the music guide you, manipulate you, or hold back?
- Was silence doing something important there?

## Editing, Rhythm, and Structure Lens

Probe:
- pace of cuts
- duration of shots
- abrupt versus smooth transitions
- repeated structural beats
- when the film lingers or skips

Ask:
- Did the scene feel fast, delayed, suspended, or jagged?
- Was there a cut or transition that changed your reading of the moment?
- Does the film repeat a rhythm or pattern that starts to mean something?

## Selection Rules

- After a scene anchor appears, try at least one formal lens before moving upward to broad interpretation.
- If a user already noticed one formal signal, follow that signal first instead of choosing a different lens arbitrarily.
- If the interview has already covered one formal bucket well, try a different uncovered bucket before drafting.
- Use the formal lens to sharpen evidence, not to show off film vocabulary.
