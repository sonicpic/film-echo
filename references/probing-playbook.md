Use this file when the user's answers are vague, too macro, or blocked by uncertainty.
Use it especially for art-house, slow cinema, symbolic, or difficult films.

## Question Design Rules

- Ask from the smallest usable unit upward.
- Prefer observed detail before declared meaning.
- Ask one concrete thing at a time.
- When the user says "I do not know," convert the question into bounded options.
- Do not jump from one remembered moment straight to the whole theme.
- After a strong answer, decide whether to deepen, branch, compare, revisit, or pressure-test.
- If the interview begins to feel linear, deliberately switch threads and come back later.
- If the previous prompt was interpretive and a scene anchor exists, prefer a formal follow-up next.

## Micro-Units to Probe

### Image or Shot

Ask:
- What do you literally see?
- Is the camera close, far, static, or moving?
- Which corner of the frame keeps pulling your eye back?
- Does the image feel crowded, empty, cold, soft, or threatening?

### Gesture or Performance Beat

Ask:
- Was there a face, pause, glance, or hesitation that stayed with you?
- Did someone speak too softly, too late, or as if they were withholding something?
- What tiny behavior made the scene feel true or strange?

### Sound, Silence, and Rhythm

Ask:
- Do you remember music, silence, noise, or off-screen sound more clearly?
- Did the scene feel abrupt, dragging, suspended, or rhythmic?
- Was there a cut or pause that changed how you felt?

### Object, Space, or Repetition

Ask:
- Was there a room, doorway, corridor, table, bed, or recurring object that felt important?
- Did any color, texture, weather, or lighting pattern quietly shape your feeling?
- Did a repeated action gain weight over time?

### Relationship or Power

Ask:
- In that scene, who seemed to control the emotional temperature?
- Who was reaching, withdrawing, watching, or being watched?
- Did the power dynamic shift without anyone saying it out loud?

## Branch Triggers

When one of these appears, consider the listed response mandatory unless the user clearly redirects:

- The user says "I can't explain it" or "it's just a feeling":
  Move from abstraction to a concrete scene, object, gesture, or sound.
- Two consecutive turns stay at abstract judgment:
  Branch to a scene anchor or a formal lens.
- A main thematic line is already visible:
  Ask how the film formally produces that effect.
- The same detail appears twice:
  Deepen it or compare it with another moment.
- The user mentions acting, camera, music, silence, rhythm, editing, or atmosphere:
  Enter the corresponding formal lens immediately instead of returning to theme.
- Emotion and personal resonance are already rich while formal threads are thin:
  Prioritize performance, visual, sound, or editing questions next.

## Cross-Thread Prompts

Use these to keep the interview from becoming one-directional:

- Does this moment echo another scene, or does it cut against it?
- Did the same feeling return somewhere else in a different form?
- Is there a second detail that makes this first one clearer or stranger?
- Did your opinion change as the film went on?
- If we go back to the first scene you mentioned, does it mean something different now?
- Is this reaction mainly about the character, the filmmaking, or something it touches in your own life?
- What answer were you almost about to give, but did not fully say?

## Confusion Support

If the user says they did not understand the film, narrow the confusion:

- Was it the plot connection that felt unclear?
- Was it a character's motive?
- Was it the ending?
- Was it the tone, symbol, or emotional logic?
- Did you understand what happened but not why it was filmed that way?

Then ask:
- Which possibility feels least forced?
- What part feels intentionally withheld rather than simply confusing?
- What did the film still make you feel, even before you understood it?

## Art-Film Rescue Ladder

Use this when the user needs extra help:

1. Reconstruct one scene in plain terms.
2. Identify one formal choice in that scene.
3. Ask what effect that choice had.
4. Offer 2-3 tentative local readings.
5. Ask which reading fits best or least badly.
6. Only then connect the scene to the whole film.

## Dialectical Pressure Prompts

Use one or two of these when a reading feels too easy:

- What detail supports this idea most strongly?
- What detail weakens it?
- Are we mistaking atmosphere for symbolism?
- Is this a flaw in the film, or partly a limit in my own way of entering it?
- Could the film be deliberately refusing a clean answer?
- What would someone who disagrees with this reading point to?

## Stop Condition

Stop probing only when the user has moved beyond generic adjectives and can point to remembered details, uncertainty, cross-links, and stakes in a way that could genuinely support writing.
