# Film Echo Lens Bank

Use this file only after enough concrete material has been gathered.
Use it mainly for candidate angle generation and argumentative direction.
For performance, visual form, sound, directing, or editing follow-up questions, read [references/formal-lens-bank.md](formal-lens-bank.md) first.
Translate the user's material into three candidate angle sentences that feel distinct, arguable, and anchored in details already discussed.

## How to Propose Angles

- Build from the user's own wording, not from generic film-school taxonomy.
- Prefer tensions, verbs, and judgments over noun labels.
- Keep each angle to one sentence.
- Offer three options when possible: one detail-led option, one relation-or-form-led option, and one tension-or-uncertainty-led option.
- Make each option something that could anchor a full essay, not just a section heading.
- If a possible angle cannot point back to at least one remembered detail, do not use it yet.

## Common Lenses

### Theme or Question

Use when the user keeps circling one human problem, moral knot, or existential question, and there is already detail support.
Template: "This may be a piece about how the film turns X into Y."

### Relationship Dynamic

Use when the user's strongest memory is between two people, a family structure, or a power imbalance.
Template: "This may be a review about the way the film makes relationship X feel like Y."

### Character Fracture

Use when the user is fixated on a person splitting, changing, hiding, failing, or performing.
Template: "This may be a piece about why I could not stop watching X come apart into Y."

### Formal Strategy

Use when the user remembers framing, rhythm, sound, color, distance, repetition, or texture.
Template: "This may be less about what happens than about how the film uses form to make me feel Y."

### Detail or Symbol

Use when one prop, image, motif, line, or recurring gesture carries unusual weight.
Template: "This may be a review built around how detail X quietly reorganizes the whole film for me."

### Gaze, Distance, or Control

Use when who is looking, who is watched, or how close the camera stays feels central.
Template: "This may be a piece about how the film controls my distance from X until that distance itself becomes the point."

### Withholding or Ambiguity

Use when the user is bothered, fascinated, or moved by what the film refuses to explain.
Template: "This may be a review about what the film withholds from me and why that refusal matters."

### Reality Resonance

Use when the user links the film to daily life, study, family, intimacy, work, class, or social feeling.
Template: "This may be a piece about why the film keeps leaking into my own life through X."

### Contradiction or Split Response

Use when the user admires and resists the film at the same time.
Template: "This may be a review that stays inside the gap between what I admire about the film and what I still cannot surrender to."

## Selection Heuristics

Prefer angles that already have concrete support.
If the user is ambivalent, include one option that treats the ambivalence as the thesis itself.
If the user is vivid on form, include at least one form-led option.
If the user lacks confidence, include one option that protects uncertainty instead of erasing it.
If the user's first take feels too comfortable, include one option that complicates or pressures it.
If all candidate angles sound academic, rewrite them closer to the user's emotional register.
